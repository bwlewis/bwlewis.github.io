Generational Vengeance. A Parable?

In 1982 Al Gore organized a hearing on climate change in the United States House of Representatives featuring dire warnings about potential global catastrophe by James Hansen, then head of NASA Goddard. Even earlier, in 1977, Exxon scientists prepared a report that stated "... there is general scientific agreement that the most likely manner in which mankind is influencing the global climate is through carbon dioxide release from the burning of fossil fuels."

More than a generation of people have long known what is now plain to see: future generations shall inherit a planet beset by climate catastrophe. Fresh water shortages, mass global migration away from newly uninhabitable places, regular massively energetic storms, mass extinction, pestilence, suffering.

Perhaps fittingly, the current youth generation is largely immune to the effects of, if not infection by, the coronavirus. A virus highly lethal to older generations. Science tells us now that our young generation can help stop the spread of the pandemic by avoiding socializing. No school, no graduations, no travel, no bar hopping, no work even.

Should the youth believe the science? Or maybe throw caution to the wind and party on like it's 2019? I mean, after all, hardly anyone in the USA has died so far, right? Maybe it's not so bad. These projections are just estimates anyway -- the risk doesn't seem large. And besides, the youth will be fine anyway, so what's the problem here?

I wonder honestly, should the youth even really care?

— Bryan W. Lewis, March 2020
